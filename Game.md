      <h1 id="about">About</h1>
<p>Broadcast is a role-playing game in which you will direct, produce and star in your own television series. Each player will create a character that is the star of their own story-line. You will explore a major story arc for your character each season with each season lasting for a single session.</p>
<blockquote>
<h2 id="time">Time</h2>
<p>Time isn’t exactly linear in this game. On the same day one player may<br>
shoot a scene for episode 2 while another player is shooting episode<br>
16, It’s fine. There is only one fixed point in time for this game and<br>
that lies somewhere between your piles. Everything to the left is<br>
before that point and everything to the right is after that point. How<br>
much before or after is up to you. Try to think of of it like this,<br>
your left pile are are scenes that have already been shot but still<br>
need to go to post. Your right pile is still being written, this is a<br>
draft of the script and it will go that way if your character gets its<br>
way but, things are subject to changes. In this game post isn’t done<br>
on a computer its done through conversation. If something doesn’t make<br>
sense try to talk out how you can use the footage you have to make it<br>
work. If that can’t be done, do a re-shoot. Play out a pivotal scene<br>
again or make a new one to see how it turns out.</p>
</blockquote>

<h3 id="during-a-scene">During a Scene</h3>
<ol>
<li><strong>Previously…</strong> Your left card left the viewers wanting more. Remind them why they are excited to see this scene.</li>
<li><strong>Choose Theme</strong> The producer chooses a theme for this scene.</li>
<li><strong>Choose an Obstacle</strong> The player with the “Choose an obstacle” marker will select an obstacle from the previously selected focus. Pass the obstacle to the “Active Player”</li>
<li><strong>Active Player</strong> Place the received obstacle in front of you (not in a pile). Choose one:
<ul>
<li>Choose a pile</li>
<li>Choose an action</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>**** The player to left then performs the option not chosen in step 3</li>
<li><strong>Coming Soon</strong>  Your right card will happen. Give a glimpse into the future or foreshadow some event</li>
<li><strong>Pass Roles</strong> Everyone with a role card passes it one player to the right.</li>
</ol>
<h2 id="the-pitch">The Pitch</h2>
<h3 id="theme">Theme</h3>
<p>Focus is a topic of interest which exists in the context. Often these are the things agents might worry about. Each focus also has an inevitable outcome or, what would happen if no agents disrupted the focus.</p>
<h3 id="obstacle">Obstacle</h3>
<p>Obstacles provide something which must be overcome in order for an agent to achieve their goal. Each obstacle is related to a single focus.</p>
<h3 id="action">Action</h3>
<p>Actions are the means that agents may employ to overcome obstacles.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Default actions are</dt>
<dd>Attune</dd>
<dd>Command</dd>

<dd>Survey</dd>

<h3 id="create-a-pitch">Create a Pitch</h3>
<ol>
<li>All players should decide on a context statement</li>
<li>Each player creates a focus.  2.</li>
<li>For each focus, each play creates one obstacle. Each player should make 3 copies of each obstacle and place them in a pile next to the associated focus.</li>
</ol>

<h3 id="my-secret-agenda-do-not-read">MY SECRET AGENDA DO NOT READ!</h3>
<p>If you are reading this then my ploy to trick you into reading this worked.</p>
<p>Whats the difference between a role playing game and a pure story game or a pure tactical game?</p>
<p>Relationships.</p>
<p>Building them and destroying them.</p>
<p>A role-playing game is the endeavor of altering relationships to form a complex web which entangles your character in a story. To rip an entangled character from a story is to irrevocably tear a hole in the story and leaves the character without meaningful context.</p>

<p>This serves to document the game and helps create more interesting stories as the graph develops. But, why stop there? Relationships aren’t just for people. Other relationship graphs can be created for factions, magic, ideas, locations or just about anything as long as its important to the story.  Moreover, these graphs can be connected to one another to create interconnected graphs of graphs.</p>
<p>This example shows how graphs can be used to describe an existing story. What if, we could instead create a tangled messy web of relationships first? what if we could then use this graph prescriptively to generate a story. We could trace arbitrary paths in our map to see hidden stories that would otherwise go unnoticed.</p>
<p>This gets me to my secret agenda. This game may have rules to be a story telling game but, its actually a graph building game. The game as presented is a veneer on top of the graph building game. It is structured in such a way that each decision can be trivially added to a graph. Eventually, I want to develop the techniques to convert this graph into readable stories. In this theoretical other version of the game we don’t play scenes and there are no epilogues.</p>

<p>There are two separate relationship dichotomies at play.</p>
<p>The first is intrinsic vs. extrinsic. Or, whether the relationship is an intrinsic property of the world or not. Jamie IS Tyrions brother but, Daenerys CHOSE Dragons to attune herself to.</p>
<p>What are the consequences of this?</p>
<ul>
<li>An agent internal to the story has no power to change intrinsic relationships.</li>
<li>An agent internal to the story may be able to change extrinsic relationships.</li>
</ul>
<p>The second is inter-graph vs. intra-graph. Or, does the line cross one the blue borders.</p>
<p>What are the consequences of this?</p>
<ul>
<li>All verticies (boxes with words) contained in the same graph (blue box) share an implicit relationship, namely the title of the graph.</li>
<li>Vertices in different graphs have no implied relationship.</li>
</ul>
<p>Great! what does this mean for me? For starters, remember when we had one type of relationship? Now we have four types. This means we can talk about relationships with more precision later on.</p>

<h3 id="making-levers">Making levers</h3>
<blockquote>
<p>Give me a lever long enough and a fulcrum on which to place it, and I shall move the world. - Archimedes</p>
</blockquote>
<p>But what if we don’t want to move the world? We spent a lot of time getting the world exactly where it is. What if we just want to nudge our character past those guards without anyone asking questions?</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Give me an appropriately long lever and rules on when I can use it, and I shall move the narrative along. - Me, now</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Let’s make some appropriately long levers.</p>
<p>Instead of using levers, I’m going to steal some jargon from the programming world and call them an interface. These represent the things we can do as players to indirectly alter the world. We need to understand what the interface expects from us as input. Though, what an interface actually does or what impact it has can remain unknown.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>If you haven’t read <a href="#how-to-create-a-complicated-messy-relationship-graph-in-one-easy-step-repeated"> this entry</a>, now would be a good time for that. This is a visual explanation of the main game mechanic that we will be working on the interface for.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The basic idea here is as follows<br>
<s>1. We are on a journey.<br>
2. Some parts of the journey are known</s><br>
3. One part of the journey is unknown<br>
4. We are presented with an obstacle that occurs sometime during the unknown portion of our journey<br>
5. We are given a decision to make<br>
6. We look at our choices<br>
7. We make a choice<br>
8. We continue our updated journey</p>
